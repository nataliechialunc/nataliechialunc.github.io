## Why lipid profiles? 

In one of my work, I used UMAP to identify potential lipid profiles within the skin. Why? Because there are too many lipid species, and different lipids work together to maintain the skin barrier function. 
Looking at each lipid species individually may miss the link between different lipid species. 
By lipid profiles, we are looking at the lipid composition within the skin altogether at once. 
Still find it abstract? Keep on reading.

*Think it as a fruity veggie juice*
We can think of profiles like different mix of juice. A juice may contain different ingredients with different amounts of each ingredients. 

## Why bother looking at profiles when we could just look at each fruit seperately?

Here is why we need to look at "profiles" (clusters of ingredients) rather than analyzing every single fruit and vegetable individually.

1. Ingredients Work Together (Synergy)
Imagine you are judging a juice and you only measure one variable: the amount of ginger. If you find a massive spike of ginger, you might conclude that the drink will be undrinkably spicy and bitter.

However, you are missing the context. If that massive spike of ginger is accompanied by high levels of carrot and apple, it is actually a perfectly balanced, sweet immunity shot. By looking at the profile (the combination of ingredients) rather than the individual ingredient, you understand how they interact. In research, biological markers don't act alone; their combined profile tells the real story.

2. Avoiding Information Overload (Redundancy)
Suppose a juice contains spinach, kale, spirulina, and celery. If you analyze each of these individually, you are running four different tests just to figure out that the drink is green and tastes earthy.

These ingredients are highly correlated—they essentially do the same job. Instead of getting bogged down by testing every single leafy green independently, grouping them into a single "Green Base Profile" simplifies your data and reduces noise without losing any meaningful information.

3. Identifying the True "Phenotype"
If I hand you a list of 20 individual ingredients and their exact milligram measurements, it takes a lot of mental effort to figure out what you are drinking.

But if we use an algorithm to group those ingredients into profiles, we can instantly categorize the drinks into distinct groups:

- The "Citrus Detox" profile

- The "Berry Antioxidant" profile

- The "Green Energy" profile

In a clinical setting, profiling does exactly this. Instead of staring at more than hundred of individual variables, you identify the overarching "recipe." 
This allows you to say, "Indivudals with this specific lipid profile are at a higher risk," which is a far more powerful and predictive conclusion than looking at a single drop of lemon juice.

## From individual ingredient to profiling, we need UMAP
UMAP sound incredibly intimidating, but the core idea is actually quite intuitive.
It's basically a way of achieving the points above- where we reduce the complexity of the data but also find the potential profiles!
Of course, there are factors that changes the output of the UMAP 2D plot, and I won't go into details here.
